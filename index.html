Theory & Principles

The Mathematics of Invisible Force
01

What is an Electric Field?

An electric field is a physical field that exists in the space surrounding any electrically charged object. It is the mechanism by which one charged particle exerts a force on another at a distance — without any physical contact between them.

The electric field at any point in space is defined as the electrostatic force per unit positive charge that would be experienced by a small positive test charge placed at that point. It is a vector quantity — it has both magnitude and direction.

Fig 1 — Electric field radiating outward from a positive point charge in all directions
E = F / q
E = electric field strength (N/C or V/m)
F = electrostatic force on the test charge (N)
q = magnitude of the positive test charge (C)

For a single point charge Q, the field strength at distance r is given by Coulomb's Law:

E = kQ / r²
k = Coulomb's constant = 8.99 × 10⁹ N·m²/C²
Q = source charge (C) — can be + or −
r = distance from the source charge (m)
Direction: radially outward for +Q, inward for −Q

The field strength decreases with the square of distance. Double the distance → the field drops to ¼ its original value. This is the inverse-square law, the same relationship that governs gravity.

02

Electric Field Lines

Electric field lines (also called lines of force) are imaginary curves drawn through space such that the tangent at any point on the curve gives the direction of the electric field vector at that point. They were invented by Michael Faraday as a visual tool.

Fig 2 — Field lines from a positive charge showing direction, arrowheads and density near the source
  • Origin & Terminus: Lines always begin on positive charges and terminate on negative charges. If there is no negative charge nearby, they extend to infinity.
  • Direction shown by arrows: Arrowheads on the lines indicate the direction the field points — away from positive charges, toward negative charges.
  • Density indicates strength: The closer together the field lines are, the stronger the electric field in that region. Near a charge the lines are dense; far away they spread out.
  • Lines never cross: Two field lines can never intersect each other, because that would imply two different field directions at the same point, which is physically impossible.
  • Perpendicular to conductors: In electrostatic equilibrium, field lines always meet the surface of a conductor at exactly 90°.
03

Single Charges & the Dipole

The pattern of field lines depends entirely on the arrangement of charges. Understanding the two fundamental single-charge cases lets you predict the field for any configuration.

⊕ Positive Charge

Field lines radiate outward uniformly in all directions, like spokes on a wheel. The field vector at every surrounding point points away from the charge. Think of it as a source — field lines flow out of it.

⊖ Negative Charge

Field lines converge inward from all directions, terminating at the charge. The field vector at every surrounding point points toward the charge. Think of it as a sink — field lines flow into it.

Fig 3 — Electric dipole: a positive and negative charge pair with characteristic curved field lines

When a positive and negative charge are placed near each other, they form an electric dipole. The fields from both charges superimpose by vector addition at every point in space. The resulting field lines are elegant curves that leave the positive charge and curve around to terminate on the negative charge.

When two like charges (both + or both −) are near each other, the field lines from each charge push each other away. A null point (saddle point) forms exactly between them where the two fields cancel perfectly — the net electric field is zero.

04

Electric Potential & Equipotential Surfaces

The electric potential V at a point in space is defined as the work done per unit charge in moving a positive test charge from infinity to that point against the electric field. It is a scalar quantity (no direction), measured in volts (V).

V = kQ / r
V = electric potential (Volts)
k = 8.99 × 10⁹ N·m²/C²
Q = source charge (C)
r = distance from source (m)

An equipotential surface is a surface on which the electric potential is the same everywhere. No work is required to move a charge along an equipotential surface. The most important geometric relationship is:

Electric field lines are always perpendicular to equipotential surfaces. This is because moving along an equipotential does no work, meaning there is no force component along the surface — so the force (and thus the field) must be entirely perpendicular to it.

Around a point charge, equipotential surfaces are concentric spheres. For a uniform field (like between capacitor plates), they are parallel planes perpendicular to the field.

Fig 4 — Equipotential contours (dashed) always crossing field lines at right angles around a dipole
05

Superposition Principle

The principle of superposition states that when multiple charges are present, the total electric field at any point is the vector sum of the individual electric fields produced by each charge acting alone. Similarly, the total potential is the algebraic (scalar) sum of individual potentials.

E⃗_total = E⃗₁ + E⃗₂ + … + E⃗ₙ
Each E⃗ᵢ is computed independently
then added as vectors (x and y components)

This is the principle that makes our interactive simulation work. For every point on the canvas, the simulator calculates the field contribution from each charge using E = kq/r², then adds the components together to get the net field vector drawn at that point.

06

Real-World Applications

Understanding electric fields is not just academic — it is the foundation of virtually all electrical engineering and much of modern physics. Electric field control is how we build technology.

Fig 6 — Parallel plate capacitor creating a uniform electric field between two conducting plates

Capacitors store energy in the electric field between two parallel conducting plates. The uniform field between the plates (E = V/d) stores energy with density u = ½ε₀E². This is how camera flashes, defibrillators and power supplies store and release energy.

Particle accelerators use carefully shaped electric fields to accelerate charged particles to near-light speed. The Large Hadron Collider uses electric fields to accelerate protons through billions of volts.

Cathode Ray Tubes steer electron beams using electric fields between deflection plates. The same principle is used in oscilloscopes, old televisions, and electron microscopes.
